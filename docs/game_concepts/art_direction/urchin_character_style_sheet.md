# Spherid / Drift Game Concept: Drift Character Style Sheet

**Status:** Planning sheet  
**Date:** 2026-04-24  
**Purpose:** Define the visual identity, expression system, material treatment, and state changes for Drift, the playable spherid character.

---

## 1. Character Goal

Drift should be:
- iconic,
- readable in motion,
- emotionally expressive without a face,
- visually distinct from the environment,
- simple enough to animate and render economically,
- rich enough to carry the game’s emotional arc.

The character should not depend on:
- facial animation,
- dense texture detail,
- realism,
- excessive ornament.

Its expression should come from:
- motion,
- posture,
- timing,
- panel behavior,
- subtle light and material response.

---

## 2. Core Identity

Drift should feel like a hybrid of:
- a living artifact,
- a soft creature,
- a sacred toy,
- a tiny wandering instrument.

It should read as:
- ancient but new,
- vulnerable but resilient,
- strange but lovable,
- simple in form but deep in behavior.

---

## 3. Primary Shape

### Base Silhouette

The base silhouette should remain strongly spherical and immediately recognizable.

Key qualities:
- compact,
- clean,
- iconic from a distance,
- readable against complex terrain,
- able to look still, timid, playful, or bold depending on pose and motion alone.

### Panel Language

Panels or shell segments should do most of the expressive work.

They should support:
- tiny breathing-like micro-adjustments,
- emotional compression and expansion,
- resonance pulses,
- locomotion deformation,
- state shifts during revelations.

The panels should not make the silhouette noisy at rest.

At rest:
- Drift should feel elegant and contained.

In action:
- the shell should animate into more dynamic and characterful shapes.

---

## 4. Material Direction

Drift should not look metallic in a hard industrial sense and should not look like a glossy plastic toy.

Recommended material blend:
- matte base body,
- soft pearlescent undertones,
- subtle seam or panel glow channels,
- selective emissive response during resonance,
- slightly sacred, ceramic-organic finish.

Possible feel references:
- glazed stone,
- shell,
- seed casing,
- polished bone-ceramic,
- moonlit fruit skin,
- artifact enamel.

The material should support beauty under many lighting conditions without requiring extreme texture detail.

---

## 5. Color Direction

### Base Character Palette

Drift’s default palette should be restrained and flexible enough to sit inside many environments.

Recommended base:
- soft ivory,
- warm pale gray,
- faint pearl,
- muted shell tone.

This keeps the body readable without fighting every biome palette.

### Accent Color System

The main color variation should come from:
- seam glow,
- resonance channels,
- subtle internal light,
- state-based tint shifts.

Accent colors can include:
- pale cyan,
- gold-white,
- soft teal,
- faint rose,
- rare full-spectrum shimmer in visionary states.

Avoid making the base character overly saturated in normal play.

---

## 6. Expression Without A Face

Drift needs a strong non-facial performance language.

### 6.1 Rest Pose

At rest Drift should be able to read as:
- shy,
- listening,
- safe,
- asleep,
- uncertain.

Visual cues:
- slight inward compression,
- soft panel settling,
- reduced glow,
- centered weight.

### 6.2 Curious Pose

Visual cues:
- slight lean,
- asymmetrical micro-expansion,
- tiny exploratory roll adjustments,
- brief pulse flicker.

### 6.3 Joyful Motion

Visual cues:
- lively rhythmic pulses,
- more elastic movement,
- confident rebound,
- fuller resonance bloom,
- cleaner flowing line through terrain.

### 6.4 Fear Or Vulnerability

Visual cues:
- tighter compression,
- lower profile,
- abrupt stillness,
- reduced visible glow,
- shelter-seeking motion.

### 6.5 Reverence Or Revelation

Visual cues:
- stillness before bloom,
- symmetrical opening,
- soft internal light rise,
- cleaner more centered posture,
- slower more deliberate pulses.

Expression should always remain subtle and legible, never exaggerated into cartoon slapstick.

---

## 7. Motion Personality

Drift’s animation language should evolve over the game.

### Early Game

Movement should feel:
- tentative,
- slightly awkward,
- soft,
- effortful,
- charming.

### Mid Game

Movement should feel:
- playful,
- responsive,
- increasingly confident,
- rhythm-aware.

### Late Game

Movement should feel:
- graceful,
- fast,
- fluent,
- light,
- musically intentional.

The visual arc of movement is part of the narrative.

---

## 8. Resonance State Design

Resonance is one of the main character-state visuals.

### Idle Resonance

Minimal:
- faint seam glow,
- soft internal shimmer,
- barely perceptible body vibration.

### Active Resonance

More visible:
- traveling seam light,
- harmonic pulse expansion,
- local particle emission,
- subtle body-frequency oscillation.

### Fine Resonance / Gentle State

Visual emphasis:
- delicate controlled light,
- low-amplitude pulse lines,
- very clean, restrained response.

### Strong Resonance / Transform State

Visual emphasis:
- broader internal bloom,
- clear panel channel lighting,
- stronger particle response,
- visible environmental coupling.

The player should always be able to read when the urchin is:
- idle,
- gently listening,
- actively resonating,
- overstressed,
- in revelation alignment.

---

## 9. Damage, Stress, And Fatigue Readability

Even if the game is not combat-driven, the urchin may need readable stress states.

Possible visual signals:
- dimmed seam lights,
- irregular pulse rhythm,
- more erratic panel timing,
- loss of smoothness,
- temporary material dulling,
- slight wobble instability.

Avoid:
- gore,
- heavy damage realism,
- brittle HUD-driven condition indicators.

Stress should read emotionally and physically, not graphically.

---

## 10. Visionary / Revelation State

During rare revelation interludes, the urchin should not become unrecognizable.

Instead, the character should intensify elegantly:
- cleaner silhouette,
- more symmetrical panel opening,
- expanded internal glow,
- faint translucency or halo effect,
- harmonized color response with the world.

These states should feel like:
- the urchin becoming more itself,
not
- the urchin turning into a different creature.

---

## 11. Contrast Against Environments

Because the world will often be rich and atmospheric, the urchin needs reliable visibility rules.

Recommendations:
- maintain a stable value range that separates it from most ground surfaces,
- use seam glow for clarity in darker biomes,
- keep the silhouette clean enough to read while rolling quickly,
- ensure bloom does not wash out edge readability.

The character should read clearly in:
- Moss Cathedral shadow,
- Marble Dunes sunlight,
- Bloom Caverns darkness,
- River Mirrors reflection,
- Wind Arches high-speed exposure.

---

## 12. Scale And Camera Considerations

The character should hold up visually at:
- close emotional framing,
- mid-distance gameplay framing,
- scenic wide shots.

That means:
- simple silhouette,
- a few bold readable features,
- no dependence on tiny details,
- expressive motion timing doing part of the work normally done by face acting.

---

## 13. Production Rules

To keep the character efficient:

- Keep the base material system compact.
- Use a small number of controlled visual states.
- Let animation and shader response create expressiveness.
- Avoid too many unique surface details that will be lost during play.
- Make panel behavior and seam lighting the core expressive tools.

The character should be economical to produce but deep in feel.

---

## 14. Final Recommendation

Drift should be designed as:

**A clean, iconic, pearl-matte spherical pilgrim with expressive panel motion, subtle luminous seams, and emotionally legible state changes driven by movement and resonance.**

It should feel:
- tender early,
- playful midgame,
- luminous and graceful late,
- and unmistakably itself in every biome.
